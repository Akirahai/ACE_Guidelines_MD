## 1. Overview
**Objective**
To support management of generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), for achieving remission and reducing relapse.

Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) is characterised by excessive anxiety and worry across multiple domains of life, with additional physical and/or cognitive symptoms, that lead to significant distress and functional impairment. GAD is reported by patients to have the most significant impact on quality of life compared to other mental health disorders studied in Singapore.

Enhancing the capacity of community-based mental health care through a tiered care model is a focus area of the 2023 National Mental Health and Well-being Strategy. Accordingly, this ACE Clinical Guidance (ACG) aims to inform the clinical management of adults (18 years old and above) in primary and generalist care with a diagnosis of GAD, including those with comorbid depression or other anxiety disorders. Guidance on major depressive disorder (MDD) can be found in the ACG Major depressive disorder – achieving and sustaining remission.

## 2. Scope & Target Audience
**Scope**
Non-pharmacological and pharmacological management of GAD in adults.

**Target Audience**
This clinical guidance is relevant to all healthcare professionals caring for patients with diagnosed GAD, especially those in primary or generalist care.

## 3. Statement of Intent
> This ACE Clinical Guidance (ACG) provides concise, evidence-based recommendations and serves as a common starting point nationally for clinical decision-making. It is underpinned by a wide array of considerations contextualised to Singapore, based on best available evidence at the time of development. ## 4. Definitions & Key Classifications
**GAD Severity Classification**
Clinicians should assess whether a patient's condition is mild, moderate, or severe, based on the intensity of symptoms and the impact on subjective distress and daily functioning.

**GAD-7 Scoring & Severity Levels**
The GAD-7 scale provides an initial, brief assessment of intensity or frequency of symptoms. Scores are grouped using 5, 10, and 15 as cutoffs:
- **Mild GAD:** 5–9 points
- **Moderate GAD:** 10–14 points
- **Severe GAD:** 15–21 points

**Functional Impairment Assessment**
Functioning can be assessed through the additional functional question in GAD-7 or more systematically through rating scales such as the World Health Organization Disability Assessment Schedule 2.0. Clinicians should consider difficulties in daily activities (e.g. work, education, household), self-care, social interactions, and participation in community, compared to the patient's premorbid state. In some cases, the degree of functional impairment may be marked and more severe than the GAD-7 score suggests.

## 5. Assessment / Diagnosis
Assessing the severity of symptoms, degree of functional impairment, and other patient factors informs treatment planning. Patient factors should be explored to tailor the treatment approach:

**Patient Needs, Preferences, and Readiness**
Discuss and agree on:
- Goals of treatment
- Preference between treatment modalities
- Willingness and ability to engage in psychological treatment
- Ability to adhere to regular medication

**Comorbid Mental Health Conditions**
- First-line treatment options for GAD overlap with MDD and other anxiety disorders.
- Consider involving specialist care for patients with other mental health conditions such as comorbid personality disorders, eating disorders, substance use disorder, schizophrenia, or bipolar disorder.

**Comorbid Physical Illnesses**
- If pharmacological treatment is chosen, select medications with proven safety in comorbid physical illnesses and less potential for drug-drug interactions.
- Adjust dose for renal and hepatic impairment, if appropriate.

**Social and Environmental Factors**
Identify adverse life events, relationship conflicts, and stressors at home, workplace, or school. Some stressors can be addressed through social care and assistance.

**Substance Use**
Specialised, integrated treatment may be suitable to address both substance use (e.g. alcohol, nicotine, prescription medications, or illicit drugs) and GAD.

**GAD Treatment History and Past Episodes**
Use past responses to medication or psychological treatment to help guide choice of treatment.

**Risk of Harm to Self and Others**
- **Risk of harm to self:** GAD is associated with an increased risk of having suicidal thoughts, planning, and attempts. Use an empathetic patient-centred approach to identify any thoughts of suicide and planning. Clinical judgment is essential, and can be informed by questions developed to assess risk. An example is the Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) screener. In devising a safety plan, it is also important to understand the patient's risk factors (e.g. past self-harm, hopelessness), and protective factors (e.g. social support).
- **Risk of harm to others:** Assess if the patient has any thoughts or plans to harm others.
- Over the GAD treatment course, the safety plan may need to be revisited.
- Monitor closely for emergent suicidal thoughts and behaviour when initiating any antidepressant medication.
- Collaborate with other providers or family/caregivers to ensure support is in place.

## 6. Management
Treatment for GAD is broadly categorised into psychological, pharmacological, or a combination of both modalities. General care and support complement pharmacological or psychological treatments.

**General Care and Support**
- **Psychoeducation and self-help:** Provide psychoeducation to all patients (and caregivers if applicable) on the disorder, treatment options, potential benefits and adverse effects, and support channels. Provide access to self-help materials, such as through Mind SG and Mindline.sg, and helplines.
- **Social services and community resources:** Refer to social services for financial assistance, social support, unemployment support, housing issues, and other stressors, depending on need. Patients and caregivers may also be able to find support schemes and services through SupportGoWhere. Connect patients with support groups and peer support specialists.
- **Lifestyle practices:** Advise structured physical exercise (e.g. aerobic or resistance training or both) for patients not meeting national physical activity guidelines. Suggest mind-body practices (e.g. yoga, taichi). Suggest stress management techniques. Advise reducing caffeine intake. Promote good sleep hygiene.

**Psychological Treatment**
Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) has the most empirical support for GAD. Apart from traditional CBT, other approaches include third-wave CBT (e.g. acceptance and commitment therapy, mindfulness-based cognitive therapy) and transdiagnostic approaches. Common elements of CBT-based approaches are psychoeducation, cognitive restructuring, behavioural techniques, relaxation, stress and worry management, problem-solving, and relapse prevention. Sleep management may be relevant if sleep is a concern.
- **Delivery format:** Face-to-face therapy has the strongest evidence base for GAD. Guided internet-delivered therapy is emerging as an alternative for suitable individuals with mild to moderate conditions. Applied relaxation, a non-CBT-based approach, also has some evidence of efficacy.

**Pharmacological Treatment**
SSRIs and SNRIs have anxiolytic effects and are established first-line medications for GAD. The choice between individual SSRIs and SNRIs can be guided by factors like the patient’s medical history, concomitant medications, risk of serious adverse effects, and hepatic or renal impairment.
- **SSRI and SNRI medications:** Take time to work (4–8 weeks adequate trial). Start at a low dose to minimise initial anxiety increase. Common adverse effects include changes in appetite/weight, restlessness, abnormal dreams, nausea/vomiting, dizziness, sexual dysfunction, sleep disturbances. SNRIs may increase blood pressure and heart rate. Monitor closely for emergent suicidal thoughts/behaviour during initial months and dose changes, especially in patients <25 years or with pre-existing suicide risk.
- **Alternatives:** If SSRIs/SNRIs are not suitable or poorly tolerated, effective alternatives include agomelatine, mirtazapine (off-label), and pregabalin. Agomelatine carries a risk of liver injury (requires LFT monitoring). Mirtazapine may cause sedation and weight gain. Consider specialist input for pregabalin.
- **Other anxiolytics:** Hydroxyzine may be useful in mild GAD or as an adjunct (risk of anticholinergic effects, excessive sleepiness, QTc prolongation). Benzodiazepines may be used short-term for severe acute anxiety (see Recommendation 7).
- **Complementary and alternative medicine:** Emerging trial data with lavender oil and ashwagandha, but more evidence needed. Check for use during medication history to pre-empt herb-drug interactions (e.g. St John's Wort increases serotonin syndrome risk with SSRIs).

### Recommendation 1 — Select treatment approach by assessing severity and patient factors
> Select the treatment approach by assessing GAD severity and other factors, taking into account the needs, preferences, and readiness of the patient.

### Recommendation 2 — Mild GAD management
> For patients with mild GAD:
> a) Consider CBT-based psychological treatments as first-line.
> b) Consider medication if psychological treatments are not feasible or acceptable.

CBT-based psychological treatments are preferred when GAD severity is mild, as they are associated with fewer adverse effects compared to pharmacotherapy. However, they may not be feasible or acceptable when:
- The healthcare professional assesses a need for, or the patient prefers, initiating treatment sooner.
- The healthcare professional assesses that some symptomatic improvement is required before the patient can adequately engage in psychological treatment.
- The patient is unwilling to engage in psychological treatment.
- The patient is unable to attend or commit to regular therapy sessions.
- The patient is unable to participate in or understand tasks for therapy sessions.

Medication options for mild GAD are either an SSRI or SNRI medication or a trial of hydroxyzine (with a plan to start CBT-based psychological treatment or SSRI or SNRI, if ineffective). Hydroxyzine has limited usefulness for comorbid depression symptoms and is less suitable for elderly patients due to significant anticholinergic effects.

### Recommendation 3 — Moderate GAD management
> For patients with moderate GAD:
> a) Offer a CBT-based psychological treatment or an SSRI/SNRI medication.
> b) Consider a combination of both modalities if supported by clinical need.

As either psychological treatment or an SSRI or SNRI medication can be offered first-line for moderate GAD, various factors unique to each patient's situation must be considered. Circumstances that favour combination treatment include:
- Presence of comorbid MDD, panic disorder, or social anxiety disorder
- Previous treatment response
- Intense or prolonged psychosocial stressors
- Poor social support
- Marked functional impairment

### Recommendation 4 — Severe GAD management
> For patients with severe GAD, offer a combination of CBT-based psychological treatment and SSRI/SNRI medication as first-line.

Research on combination treatment approaches for GAD is limited. The potential benefits need to be weighed against higher treatment burden. When combination treatment is not feasible or acceptable, either CBT-based psychological treatment or SSRI or SNRI medication can be offered as alternatives. Psychiatrist-led services are preferred for severe GAD.

### Recommendation 5 — Management of suboptimal response to initial treatment
> If treatment does not achieve adequate response in patients with GAD, assess possible reasons before considering modifying treatment or seeking specialist advice.

Assess response, evaluate patient adherence, and monitor for adverse effects. For SSRIs/SNRIs, assess full benefit after 4–8 weeks. For psychological treatment, evaluate at the midpoint of the planned course.
**Assessing response:** Evaluate significant improvement in symptoms and functioning. A Clinical Global Impressions-Improvement score >2 or <20% improvement on GAD-7 can inform inadequate response.
**Possible reasons to evaluate:** Ongoing psychosocial stressors, suboptimal treatment adherence, misdiagnosis/other mental health conditions, comorbid conditions mimicking GAD (e.g. hyperthyroidism, arrhythmia). Optimal therapeutic doses are not well-established; adjust based on early response, tolerability, and patient profile.
**Modifying treatment:** If initial treatment is ineffective, consider approaches in Table 2. Seek specialist involvement if response remains inadequate after two trials, or earlier if needed.

### Recommendation 6 — Management to reduce risk of relapse
> Continue treatment with an SSRI or SNRI for at least six months after achieving remission.

Continuation of the same dose for at least another 6 months (up to 12 months) leads to lower relapse rates.
**Assessing remission:** Significant improvement from baseline, no longer meeting diagnostic criteria with minimal symptoms, restoration of premorbid functioning and improved quality of life. Tailor strategies to stay well (psychoeducation, self-help, social support, lifestyle, regular follow-up).
**Role of psychological treatment in preventing relapse:** Evidence for alternative options after remission on medication is limited.
**Considerations for continuing or discontinuing antidepressants:** Discuss with patient. Consider history of relapse, adverse effects, ongoing stressors, comorbid conditions, social support, patient preference. Gradually reduce dose to minimise discontinuation symptoms.

### Recommendation 7 — Use of benzodiazepines in primary and generalist care
> Do not routinely prescribe benzodiazepines as first-line treatment for GAD.

While onset is faster than SSRIs/SNRIs, prescribing requires careful assessment due to dependence, tolerance, misuse, addiction, and withdrawal risks. Short-term use can be considered for severe acute anxiety. Their role in treatment-resistant GAD is beyond this guidance. Limited studies support adjunct use for initial weeks of antidepressant therapy; alternatives should be considered first. If required, short-term use can be considered for distressing symptoms.
**Principles of prescribing:** Assess history of substance abuse, current opioid/alcohol/CNS depressant use, respiratory disease. Consider daytime sedation/cognitive impairment risk, especially in elderly. Use lowest effective dose for shortest duration (up to 2–4 weeks), as-needed dosing. Educate on proper use, review for dependence/tolerance. Consider specialist assessment if inadequate relief or difficulties stopping.
**Patient communication points:** Inform dose, frequency, duration, indication. Advise on adverse effects, tolerance, dependence, addiction. Explain lowest effective dose/shortest duration. Inform not to take with alcohol or CNS depressants.

## 7. Monitoring & Follow-Up
- Follow-up occurs after an adequate trial of treatment (e.g. 4–8 weeks of an SSRI or SNRI medication, or at the midpoint of the planned psychological treatment course).
- Monitor progress towards remission.
- Monitor closely for emergent suicidal thoughts and behaviour when initiating any antidepressant medication.
- Review safety plans over the treatment course.
- For discontinuation: Gradually reduce dose to minimise discontinuation symptoms. Patients can be made aware that discontinuation is often associated with distinctive symptoms (e.g. electric sensations, dizziness, depersonalisation), which usually occurs within hours or days of reducing doses or stopping the medication.

## 8. Specialist Referral
Consider specialist involvement for:
- Comorbid personality disorders, eating disorders, substance use disorder, schizophrenia, or bipolar disorder
- Neurodevelopmental disorders (e.g. intellectual disability, autism spectrum disorder, ADHD)
- High risk of harm to self or others
- Suboptimal response after two trials of treatment, or earlier if needed (worsening/new symptoms, clinical need, tolerability issues)
- Complex mental health needs or treatment-resistant GAD
- Benzodiazepine dependence/tolerance issues or difficulties reducing/stopping
- Cases requiring specialist input for pregabalin use

## 9. Special Populations / Conditions
**Elderly**
- Assess balance of benefits and risks individually due to age-related changes, disability, and medical comorbidities.
- CBT-based approach has been shown to be effective; modifications for cognitive/learning abilities may be needed.
- If prescribing medication, start at low doses and monitor closely due to higher sensitivity to adverse effects.

**Perinatal**
- See Supplement 3 for principles of care for pregnant and postpartum women.

**Neurodevelopmental Disorders**
- Patients may have atypical presentation of GAD.
- Involve specialist care in assessment and treatment planning, as needed.
- Interventions should be adapted to the person's needs (e.g. developmental level, communication skills).

**Descriptive Summary**
This guideline outlines the management of Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD), with goals of promoting remission and reducing relapse risk. Treatment selection is based on severity (Mild, Moderate, Severe) and patient factors, starting with CBT-based psychological treatments or SSRIs/SNRIs. General care, psychoeducation, and lifestyle practices are recommended alongside specific treatments. Follow-up occurs after 4-8 weeks, with management branching based on response. For patients on antidepressants who achieve remission, treatment should continue for at least six months before considering discontinuation. Benzodiazepines are not recommended as first-line treatment.
